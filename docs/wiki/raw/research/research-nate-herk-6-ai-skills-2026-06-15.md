# Digest — Nate Herk, "Learn These 6 AI Skills Now (Before AI Replaces You)"

~18 min. The thesis: you don't have to switch careers or start a business to stay ahead of AI. Six skills future-proof you in whatever role you're in. The striking part for me: five of the six are things the AIOS + BGD pivot are already built on. This is less "new ideas" and more "outside confirmation of the operating model, with sharper language for it."

## TL;DR — the six skills

1. **Become the AI person** — be the relative AI expert in your circle; pick one tool, get genuinely good, apply it to a real workflow.
2. **Taste and judgment** — as output improves, the trap is trusting it blindly. Your name is signed to AI work. Build taste deliberately.
3. **Context engineering** — prompting fades, context endures. Feed AI your private, non-public context. (Nate calls his the "AI OS.")
4. **Iteration speed** — fastest iterator wins; every loop is data. Plus the discipline of defining "done" against one metric.
5. **Build your own Jarvis** — always-on systems that self-trigger, AND knowing when a task needs an agent vs a plain deterministic workflow.
6. **Unemployment insurance** — multiple AI-powered income streams off one passion ("one passion, multiple branches"); build in public to be discoverable.

## The six, with Nate's practical move for each

### 1. Become the AI person
Relative, not absolute. You don't need to be the best engineer alive — just know more than your circle, and be visible about it. The opportunities open *before* there's a formal title: someone says "we need a person for this," and you want to be the name that comes up. IBM's 2026 CEO study: 85% of CEOs say every functional leader must become a tech expert in their domain, not just IT. **Move:** pick one main tool (Nate uses Claude), use it to deliver real ROI on one weekly workflow, document before/after. Don't switch careers — find the AI-native version of the career you already have.

### 2. Taste and judgment
The better AI gets, the more tempting "good enough" becomes. The joke he tells: one side turns a bullet into a polished email, the other side turns the email back into one bullet. Everyone's transforming, nobody's reading. Danger, because **your name is signed to the output** — you get the credit or the blame regardless of who/what wrote it. The tell he names: **em dashes** ("if something goes out from me with five em dashes, people know I didn't write it"). **Move:** study the best work in your field, save a personal library of examples you like, ask AI *why* something is good, and feed every correction back into your instructions. "AI can generate the work. Taste is deciding what deserves your name."

### 3. Context engineering
Prompt engineering is fading as models improve; context engineering is durable. Karpathy (just joined Anthropic): "the delicate art and science of filling the context window with just the right information." Prompts = how you ask; context = what the AI actually knows. Nate built his **"AI OS"** holding everything in his head — meeting transcripts, his YouTube catalog, DMs/Slack, email — "it knows what's going on in my world better than I do." Running joke: if you can't reach him, message his AI OS for a faster, better answer. **Move:** stop opening blank chats; spin up a Claude project / custom GPT loaded with real context (product docs, calendar, copy that worked, copy that flopped). The intern analogy: onboard it or it's just a smart intern guessing. Your private IP/context is what makes outputs *not* generic — garbage in, garbage out.

### 4. Iteration speed
In the AI era the fastest iterator wins, because every iteration is more data to make your prompts/context/agents better. Bike-teaching analogy: you calibrate run by run, then slowly let go — you rarely one-shot anything; by the 15th kid the process is a science. **Move:** master keyboard shortcuts, use voice input (he plugs Glydo), rapid-prototype the ugly version fast, get out of POC. The other half is **knowing when to stop**: give yourself a North Star, tie one automation to one specific business metric, and define "done" *before* you build (tickets resolved/day, qualified appts/week, refund % down). Then move to maintenance. A clear definition of done is what stops you scope-creeping on yourself.

### 5. Build your own Jarvis
Tony Stark doesn't type prompts all day — Jarvis runs in the background, notices things, pings him, kicks off tasks unprompted. Distinct from skill 3: context engineering teaches the AI what you know; this teaches it to *act* without you being the trigger. **Move:** audit your day for predictable triggers (a type of email, every Monday, a new CRM lead) — each is handoff-able. Catch: removing yourself raises risk, so the system has to be airtight. The load-bearing sub-skill: **agent vs workflow.** Vending machine (deterministic, cheap, never breaks) vs slot machine (AI agent — variable, costs more, fails in surprising ways). "Pull Stripe revenue every morning, post to Slack" = workflow, no agent. "Read messy inbound emails, understand intent, draft tailored replies" = needs AI. Two questions per task: (1) do I need to trigger this, or can it self-fire? (2) does this step need AI, or would a Python script / no-code do it cheaper with less risk? Default to the simplest thing that works. The elite version of "being the AI person" is being the one who says "we don't actually need AI here."

### 6. Unemployment insurance
Hot take he's confident becomes normal: build multiple AI-powered income streams so no single employer/client can take you out. Old model = one job, all eggs in one basket. New model = **job stacking** (day job + a couple AI-leveraged side streams), because AI lets one person do what took a team of five. Don't stack five unrelated domains — that's burnout. Do **"one passion, multiple branches"**: same expertise/North Star, packaged different ways (course, niche newsletter, micro-SaaS, consulting). Pick one, go hard until momentum, then branch. Caveats: check your contract/non-competes, disclose. **Move:** default is **build in public** — experiment, ship small, share wins and losses, build a tiny brand around the work you already do, so you become discoverable (increasingly *by AI search interfaces*, not just people). If public isn't you: quiet consulting, a faceless newsletter, a product you sell without showing your face.
