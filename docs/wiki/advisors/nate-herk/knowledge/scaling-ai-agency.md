# The Playbook for a $100M AI Agency

**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8ktcSaSTvxk
**Published:** 2026-05-25
**Type:** YouTube video — 1h 44m interview between Nate Herk and Devin Karns (co-founder/CEO of Custom AI Studio)

## Key Frameworks and Patterns

### The 2027 Survival Filter

Devin's central thesis: most AI work being sold today won't survive 2027. The value of doing development — writing automations, wiring APIs, building workflows — is trending toward zero. The agencies that survive and scale are the ones that shift from doing technical work to delivering business transformation. The distinction: AI is not its own category. It will seep into every vertical until it's invisible infrastructure. The agencies that built an identity purely on "we do AI" will be commoditized. The ones that built identity on "we transform operations in [industry X] using AI" will compound.

The surviving positioning is: **AI as the unique mechanism, business consulting as the actual service.**

### The Revenue Multiple Thesis

This is why the $100M exit target shapes every strategic decision. A pure AI readiness consulting firm making $2M/year might sell for $2M — a 1x multiple on a services business. But a firm making $6M/year with repeatable processes, documented frameworks, and verifiable business outcomes can sell for 5x — $30M. The difference isn't just revenue. It's systematization, repeatability, and proof that the business can run without the founder. Building for exit means building systems, not capacity.

The implication: every client engagement should be designed to produce a documented, repeatable artifact that increases firm value — not just completed work.

### Mid-Market Sweet Spot

Devin and Nate converge on the mid-market ($10M–$250M revenue companies) as the prime ICP:
- Large enterprises have 18-month procurement cycles, compliance theater, and internal politics that kill momentum.
- SMBs don't have budget, and deal size doesn't justify delivery cost.
- Mid-market has real problems, real budget, real decision-makers who can say yes in a meeting, and enough organizational complexity to create recurring needs.

Ironically, enterprises are often *less* accessible than mid-market companies despite being better known targets. Mid-market buyers are under-served precisely because most AI agencies chase enterprise logos or chase volume at the SMB level.

### Framework Positioning vs. Vendor Positioning

Nate surfaces a pattern he's been building across his YouTube content: agencies that lead with named frameworks win more trust and charge more than agencies that lead with capabilities or deliverables. When you say "I do AI automations," you're a vendor. When you say "We run our Operational AI Diagnostic to find your top three ROI opportunities, then build against a signed blueprint," you're a consultant with a process.

Nate ties this directly to brand consistency: the repeated patterns, the named frameworks, the consistent vocabulary — they give the whole practice a feel of coherence. Clients buy confidence, not capability. The framework is the signal that you've done this before.

An industry-specific version of this is even more powerful. A firm that has niched their process to, say, manufacturing operations or distribution networks creates instant trust: the client assumes the firm has solved their specific problem before.

### The Five-Chapter Playbook (Devin's Path)

Structured around the video's timestamp chapters:

**Chapter 1 — The Inflection Point:** AI is becoming infrastructure in every vertical. The opportunity window is 2-3 years before commoditization closes it. The window is open right now for people who can package AI services with business consulting rigor.

**Chapter 2 — The Economics:** The gap between a lifestyle business and an exit-worthy business is not just revenue. It's margins, repeatability, and systems. Custom implementations with high per-client economics (e.g., $300K/year per law firm client) dwarf what's possible in SaaS-like pricing for equivalent complexity. The economics of custom AI work are fundamentally different from SaaS.

**Chapter 3 — The Big Bet:** Committing to a niche or vertical is the strategic bet that unlocks premium positioning and referral networks. Trying to serve everyone keeps you in vendor mode. The big bet is: pick a vertical, own it, build the documented process, and become the person in that category.

**Chapter 4 — Starting From Zero:** Devin's origin story. Started by learning the tools, finding first clients, and doing the work before building the brand. His advice for people starting: become tool-agnostic rather than betting on one platform. The core skills that transfer are creative problem solving and data manipulation — not fluency in any single low-code tool. For first revenue: charge $50 to teach someone Claude for an hour. That person knows someone who needs more. That's the referral flywheel origin point.

**Chapter 5 — The Playbook:** Delivery process matters. Discovery workshops and written blueprint documents are the mechanisms that close deals and set client expectations. The deliverable before the engagement is the engagement opener. A signed blueprint gives you scope, gives the client confidence, and gives the firm a repeatable artifact to systematize.

**Chapter 6 — Resilience and the Latter:** The long game requires consistency over short-term cash optimization. Cutting corners to optimize for the next check costs you reputation, which is much harder to rebuild than revenue. The agencies that scale are the ones that stay integrity-consistent across client engagements.

### Five Things Devin Wished He Knew Sooner

1. **Commit to what you're building.** Lifestyle business, product company, or exit-track firm — each requires a completely different set of decisions. Hopping between orientations is the #1 cause of stalled agencies. Pick one and build for it without guilt.

2. **Certification and skills as a door opener.** Don't wait until you feel expert enough. Use what you've already built as proof. His framing: "Look at all this stuff I've learned and doubled down on — that is the certification." The compounding investment in expertise IS the credential.

3. **Teach before you sell.** The lowest-friction first engagement is teaching someone what you know (even for $50/hr). It establishes trust, surfaces what they actually need, and almost always generates a referral or a larger engagement. Selling is harder than teaching.

4. **Reputation compounds; short-term cash doesn't.** Every corner cut is visible to the client. Optimizing for the check damages the referral network that would have generated 10x that check over time.

5. **Distribution matters as much as building.** Most technical people are better builders than they are distributors. The agency that wins long-term invests in referral networks, partnerships, content, or a sales channel — not just better technical output.

## Positions and Opinions

- Most AI work being sold in 2026 won't exist as a separate category by 2027-2028. AI will be subsumed into every vertical's standard practice.
- The value of pure development (building automations, connecting APIs) trends toward zero. The value of business transformation consulting — with AI as the mechanism — compounds.
- Mid-market ($10M-$250M) is the right ICP. Enterprises are less accessible than they appear. SMBs are a volume game with thin margins and high churn.
- A $2M/year consulting business might sell for $2M. A $6M/year systematized firm can sell for $30M. Build for the multiple, not just the revenue.
- Most people who think they want to run an agency don't actually want the sales function. Honest self-assessment on this early saves years of misalignment.
- The agency model is a valid research lab for building a product — the services revenue funds the product development and the client work surfaces the real product-market fit signal.
- Named frameworks and documented processes are the difference between vendor and consultant positioning. Agencies that win consistently are the ones with proprietary language for what they do.
- Niche > generalist. An industry-specific process creates instant credibility and referral velocity that generic AI agencies can't match.
- Integrity in delivery is the long-term moat. Short-term optimization for the check is visible and costs you the referral network.
