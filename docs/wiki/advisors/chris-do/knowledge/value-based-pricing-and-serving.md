# Stop Selling, Start Serving: Pricing Without Fear (Adobe MAX Whiteboard Session)

**URL:** https://thefutur.com/watch/stop-selling-start-serving-price-your-work
**Published:** 2024 (Adobe MAX presentation)
**Type:** Video/article (full whiteboard session, written editorial companion)

## Key Frameworks and Patterns

### The Anatomy of Pricing Anxiety — The Broken Process

Chris Do dissects the standard creative professional sales process as fear-driven and structurally broken. The typical flow:

1. **Initial Contact** — referral or inquiry comes in
2. **First Call (Fit Check)** — 30-minute call to understand needs; money is avoided
3. **The Retreat** — creative takes time to "think" and prepare a bid, adding hours or days of delay
4. **Second Call (The Proposal)** — formal presentation walking the client through deliverables to justify price
5. **The Verdict** — yes, no, or "maybe" (which he calls a "time-vampire" worse than a definitive no)

His core diagnosis: the delay between first contact and price reveal is a "breeding ground for anxiety." He uses the roller coaster metaphor — the slow climb before the drop is more terrifying than the drop itself. Every day you delay, you extend that climb for both parties.

### Rip the Bandage Off — Price on the First Call

The corrective: have the price conversation immediately, on the first call. Do not retreat, do not prepare a formal proposal, do not delay. The longer you wait, the more fear compounds on both sides. His position is that most creatives use the "I'll think about it" retreat as an anxiety-avoidance mechanism — and it makes everything worse.

### The Fundamental Reframe: Buyer Determines Value, Seller Determines Price

Do's core pricing principle, stated flatly:

**"The buyer determines value, the seller determines price."**

Your job is not to convince a client of your value. That is not possible. The client determines what something is worth to them — your job is to discover that, then set your price in relation to it. Selling becomes easy when you know what the client finds valuable and align your price to that reality.

The seller sets price. The buyer sets value. Those are two separate jobs, and confusing them is why creatives chronically underprice or overprice.

### Stop Selling, Start Serving

The mindset shift that unlocks the framework. Do argues that selling — as creatives typically practice it — is about convincing, closing, and winning. Serving is about understanding the client's problem well enough that they convince themselves.

When you orient your entire interaction around genuinely helping them solve their problem, you remove the adversarial dynamic. The client is no longer being sold to; they are being helped. The result: they sell themselves. Do says this happens "every single time."

The posture of detached service — holding the client's needs above your own need to make a sale — converts you from vendor to strategic partner.

### Discovery as the Pricing Engine

Before price can be set, you need to understand the business case. Do's discovery framework covers:

- What are the goals?
- What are the challenges to achieving those goals?
- How will success be measured?
- Who are the customers?
- How does this affect the brand's position in the marketplace?

This is not about asking for a budget number immediately — that shuts down the conversation. It is about understanding the stakes. Once you know the stakes, the value is self-evident, and the price is a natural conclusion.

### Ideal Client Filter — Define Your Non-Negotiables

Before discovery happens, Do argues you must filter for fit. Define your deal-killers:

- Industry-based filters (e.g., won't work with certain sectors)
- Behavior-based filters: the biggest red flag is a client who says "I'll know it when I see it" — a signal of indecisiveness and endless revision cycles. The correct response: "After thinking about this, I don't think I'm a good fit for you."
- Budget filter: address money upfront to avoid wasting time with bargain shoppers. Sample script: "Before we go too far, because I value your time, do you have at least $10,000 for this project? If not, we're not going to be a good fit."

This is not arrogance — it is clarity. It signals confidence and sets expectations about the value you provide.

### Emotional Detachment in Negotiation

When a client attempts to negotiate your price down, treat it as a business tactic, not a personal insult. Reacting emotionally is "a sign of professional immaturity." Your rate is your rate. Hold the line. The value you provide exceeds the price you charge — act like it.

### Applying Value Logic to Intangibles (The Logo Objection)

The common creative objection: "This works for a website but you can't measure the ROI of a logo." Do addresses this directly. The question is not what the deliverable is — it is what the deliverable does for the client's business. Even intangible creative work connects to business outcomes: brand differentiation, employee pride, premium pricing power. The client's perception of those outcomes is the value. Ask about them.

## Positions and Opinions

- Delaying the price conversation is the single biggest structural mistake creatives make. It is a fear-avoidance behavior that compounds anxiety for both parties.
- Creatives are stuck in a "vicious cycle of undercharging and overdelivering" — rooted in fear, not market reality.
- An uncomfortable client will not make a positive spending decision. Your process should eliminate discomfort, not extend it.
- "Maybe" is worse than "no" — it is a time-vampire. The broken process is designed to produce maybes.
- You cannot convince a client of your value. Stop trying. Discover what they already value, then price to it.
- Formal proposals as a second-call ritual are a crutch, not a best practice. They exist to delay the conversation creatives are afraid to have.
- Emotional attachment to the outcome of a negotiation is a professional liability. Detachment is the correct stance.
- Filtering clients upfront — including asking about budget on the first call — is a sign of confidence, not arrogance.

## How This Applies as a Decision Lens

When advising on a business development or pricing decision, this content pushes hard on one question: are you orienting around your own fear or around the client's problem? Do's framework is a diagnostic for service businesses that underprice because they delay hard conversations, try to justify value rather than discover it, and mistake emotional detachment for coldness. Applied to Justin's AI services pivot and the Jon Liebl sales channel: lead with discovery questions that surface the client's business stakes before any number is named; set a budget threshold upfront so the conversation filters itself; price based on what success is worth to them, not on hours or deliverables; and treat any negotiation attempt as a business tactic to hold steady against, not a rejection to collapse under.
