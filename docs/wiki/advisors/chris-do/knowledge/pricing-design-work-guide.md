# Pricing Design Work: Value-Based Pricing vs. Hourly Billing

**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RKXZ7t_RiOE
**Published:** 2016-10-22
**Type:** YouTube video (36:29) — The Futur channel, 4.5M+ views

## Key Frameworks and Patterns

### Price the Client, Not the Job

The core principle: the same logo, the same deliverable, has wildly different values depending on who it's for. A mom-and-pop's logo is worth a sandwich. A Fortune 500's logo is worth a million dollars — not because the work is different, but because the risk and reach are different. You're not charging for the artifact. You're charging for what the artifact touches and what happens when it goes wrong.

Questions to determine client-appropriate price:
- What is their printing budget? How many things does this mark touch?
- What's the risk if this goes wrong — backlash, reprint costs, brand confusion?
- How big is the company? (Revenue can be reverse-engineered through the conversation.)

### The Symmetry of Logic Test

When a client insists on hourly billing, use their own logic against them. If you work faster, should you earn less? If you go over hours, should they pay more? The logic breaks down both ways. Most business people say time is their most valuable asset — so if a great logo takes 5 minutes, it's worth *more*, not less. Charging hourly punishes efficiency and rewards mediocrity.

The test: "If I could do this in 5 minutes and you love it, is it worth less than 18k? Or more — because I saved you time to go to market today?"

### The Revenue Anatomy Conversation

Rather than guessing a company's size or budget, Chris maps their revenue structure in real time — live, in the room. He identifies revenue streams (e-commerce, retail, reps, international), asks for percentage splits, then asks for rough profit margins per channel. This lets him identify where branding/marketing investment will have the highest leverage (usually the highest-margin, highest-revenue channel), without ever directly asking "what's your budget."

The math reveals itself: if improving e-commerce by 5% generates $12M in gross profit, the conversation naturally arrives at a seven-figure project budget — and the client voices the number, not Chris.

### Ask Questions Until They Say It

The meta-principle running through every negotiation tactic: never say it yourself. Ask questions until the client says it. When they say it, they believe it. This applies to value, budget, risk, priorities. The moment Chris states the value of his work, the client can resist. The moment the client states it, resistance collapses.

### The Risk Positioning Framework

Clients don't choose the best option. They choose the least risky option. To win on price, you don't need to claim you're better — you need to appear safer. Criteria clients use: years in business, case studies, referrals, office presence, team size, credit history. Social proof matters more than portfolio quality at the moment of decision. You can win a $20k logo on Day 1 of your business if you can credibly de-risk the choice.

### Confidence as the Starting Rate

The first time Chris charged $10k for strategy was the first time. He didn't ladder up from $100. He set the number, they paid it, then he raised it. The price is set by belief in value — not credentials, not track record. You can charge premium rates before you have the portfolio to prove it, if you can hold the frame.

### The Guarantee Flip

When a client demands results guarantees, offer one — at 2-3x the price. The guarantee costs more because it shifts risk. Then pro-rate: if we hit half the target, you pay half. Frame it as a 50-cent-for-a-dollar deal. Any entrepreneur rationally takes that bet. This reframes the whole conversation from "prove your value" to "what return do you want on this investment."

### Paula Scher / Picasso Principle

Experience is what you're billing — not time on task. Paula Scher drew a city logo on a cocktail napkin in 30 seconds. Her fee reflected 34 years of expertise and a six-figure education, not 30 seconds of sketching. Picasso's line: "It didn't take me 10 seconds. It took me a lifetime to draw like this." The deliverable time is irrelevant. The expertise time is what you're charging.

## Positions and Opinions

**What he advocates for:**
- Flat-rate, value-based pricing — no hourly billing, ever
- Pricing relative to the client's size, risk exposure, and revenue impact — not the task
- Asking questions rather than making claims; letting clients arrive at conclusions themselves
- Setting ambitious prices from day one — "the first time I charged $10k was the first time"
- Appearing as the least risky vendor, not the most talented
- Understanding clients' business fundamentals (revenue streams, margins, profit drivers) before proposing anything

**What he argues against:**
- Hourly billing — it punishes skill and efficiency, rewards slow work
- Pitching your value verbally — clients don't believe it when you say it
- Cutting to the budget question directly — it spooks clients and closes conversation
- Assuming you need credentials before charging premium rates
- Thinking about design in qualitative terms (looks better, more premium) when quantitative framing (2% conversion lift = $2M in revenue) wins deals

## How This Applies as a Decision Lens

When evaluating how to price any BGD service — AI automation, BrandOS platform tier, or a custom build — the question is never "how many hours will this take?" The question is "what does this unlock for the client, what's the risk if it goes wrong, and how big is the business we're solving it for?" A $750/month AI automation tier priced for a $500k/year business and a $5,000/month tier priced for a $10M business aren't different amounts of work — they're different risk equations and different revenue levers. Chris Do's framework also validates the Jon Liebl sales channel dynamic: Jon's job is to establish trust and de-risk the vendor choice before Justin ever enters the room. The credibility infrastructure (case studies, referrals, proof of system) is what makes the price hold — not Justin's ability to argue for it in the room.
