# Charging What You're Worth: Mindset, Psychology, and Premium Pricing Strategy

## Key Frameworks and Patterns

### The Financial Thermostat (Self-Concept)

Drawn from Brian Tracy's *The Psychology of Selling*, this is the central framework of the course. Your self-concept — the collection of beliefs you hold about who you are and what you're worth — functions like an internal financial thermostat. It sets a target number around which your income self-corrects within a 10–15% variance.

If you internalized $100,000 as "making it" (because that's what your father earned), you will unconsciously coast when you hit that ceiling and grind when you fall below it. The thermostat governs you. You don't override it through hustle — you override it by reprogramming the set point.

Do's framing: "What you think you are determines what you get." Income, relationships, and business outcomes all move within the gravitational pull of this internal number.

### Two Types of People (Erwin McManus Framework)

Do opens with a concept from pastor Erwin McManus: people fundamentally divide into two groups. One type runs *from pain*. The other runs *toward life and love*. Most people don't believe they're in the pain-avoidance camp — but behavior reveals it. Creatives who undercharge, seek client approval, and lower prices before being asked are running from the pain of rejection and conflict. Recognizing which mode you're operating from is the diagnostic step before any tactical change.

### The Internal Negotiation (Pre-Negotiation Against Yourself)

This is Do's most specific and repeatable pattern diagnosis. The sequence:

1. You think of a price: "$5,000."
2. Self-doubt activates: "That's too much. Why would they pay that? This is easy for me."
3. You halve it to $2,500 before saying a word.
4. The client — who is a businessperson — negotiates down from there to $2,000.
5. You accept out of fear of conflict.
6. You resent the project. The resentment bleeds into the work. The client is underwhelmed. The cycle repeats.

The key insight: **the enemy is not the client. It is the negotiation you have with yourself before the client ever sees a number.** This pattern starts in childhood — in your relationship with money, approval, and self-worth.

### Cost-Based vs. Value-Based Pricing (Egocentric vs. Empathetic)

Do calls cost-based pricing "a very self-centered, egocentric approach." When you calculate hours, effort, and experience, you are talking entirely about yourself. The client does not care what it costs you to make something — they only care what you can do for them.

The shift: stop starting with *your* inputs and start diagnosing *their* outcomes.

The reframe questions:
- "What are you trying to achieve by doing this?"
- "Why are you doing this now?"
- "What will this do for your business?"

When a client says "it would generate $10,000 more per month," the conversation is no longer about a $2,500 logo. It's anchored to a six-figure annual return. The price follows from the value of the outcome, not the cost of the labor.

Do's goal: move from a "business-to-vendor relationship" to a "business-to-business partnership."

### The Effective Hourly Rate (EHR) Exercise

Do's diagnostic exercise: map every job you've ever had, calculate the total hours invested, divide earnings by hours, and arrive at an effective hourly rate. The pattern that emerges reveals your self-concept at work — and shows the compounding effect of skill acquisition over time. His own trajectory:

- Stock boy / fry cook: ~$3.35/hr (unskilled labor)
- Inking drawings at silk-screen shop: $18/hr (applied a skill)
- Desktop publishing (Illustrator/PageMaker): $22/hr
- Art school + graphic design: $30/hr
- After Effects / animation: $50/hr
- Business coach: ~$1,500/hr effective
- Public speaker: $30,000/engagement

Each jump was the result of stacking a new marketable skill. The principle: "The more skills I acquire that are marketable to a market that is in demand, the more I was going to be able to charge."

### The Dip (Seth Godin) Applied to Skill Acquisition

Do layers Godin's "Dip" concept onto the skill-building path. When you learn something new, there is an initial high (quick visible progress), followed by a plateau of high effort and low visible reward. This valley is where most people quit — they blame the algorithm, the market, or the platform. The Dip is actually a natural barrier to entry: because most people exit, those who push through emerge with rare and valuable skills. Scarcity is created not by talent but by persistence.

Paired with Gladwell's 10,000-hour principle: mastery requires a significant commitment of time and focused effort, regardless of innate ability.

### Permission Marketing Hybrid (Attract, Don't Chase)

Do's outbound/inbound hybrid model, applied to LinkedIn or any content platform:

1. Create high-value, experience-based content (not just things you read — things you've done).
2. Offer a deeper resource at the end of the post ("Comment [keyword] and I'll send you the full guide").
3. Engage via direct message when someone comments — they've given permission.
4. Follow up a day or two later and diagnose their problem.
5. Schedule a value-first call — teach something of real value. Only transition to a potential engagement at the end.

The result: "attracting volunteers, not chasing prospects." The relationship starts warm, with trust already built.

### Lines in the Sand (Non-Negotiables and Deal-Killers)

Your strongest position in any client relationship is at the very beginning. Power only decreases from there. You must define non-negotiables upfront:

- Industry-based: industries you refuse to serve (tobacco, alcohol, etc.)
- Behavior-based: clients who can't define what they want ("I'll know it when I see it") are red flags for endless revisions

Budget qualification script: "Before we go too far, because I value your time, do you have at least $10,000 for this project? If not, we're not going to be a good fit." This isn't arrogance — it's clarity. It filters bargain shoppers early and signals confidence in your value.

Do cites Blair Enns' concept of a "minimum level of engagement" as the framework behind this.

### Reframing as a Permanent Skill

The final tool in the system. Just as an expensive frame increases the perceived value of a painting, changing the mental frame around an experience changes its emotional impact. Do uses the Bran Stark / Jaime Lannister scene from *Game of Thrones* as the example: Bran reframes a trauma as a prerequisite for who he became rather than a wound to avenge.

Practical application: every difficult client, failed project, or lost money is tuition for a lesson, not evidence of inadequacy. Do was sued early in his career and lost $30,000 to a sales rep who exploited a contract loophole. He frames both as expensive education.

Quote attributed to Gandhi: holding on to hate is like drinking poison and expecting the other person to die.

## Positions and Opinions

- **Against:** Hourly and cost-based pricing. It is self-centered, client-irrelevant, and caps earnings at a linear time-for-money exchange.
- **For:** Value-based pricing anchored to business outcomes the client cares about.
- **Against:** Negotiating against yourself before presenting a price. This is the single most destructive behavior in creative pricing.
- **For:** Stating your price and detaching from the emotional outcome. "Your rate is your rate."
- **Against:** Positioning yourself as a vendor. Creatives who see themselves as suppliers accept vendor treatment (lowest-price selection, commoditization).
- **For:** Positioning as a business partner who diagnoses and solves business problems. This changes the conversation and the client relationship.
- **Against:** Jumping straight to tactics without addressing mindset. "Mindset things are so difficult to overcome but so powerful once you unlock that." Tactics applied on top of a broken self-concept will not hold.
- **For:** Compounding marketable skills as the highest-leverage use of a creative's time. The market pays for rare, in-demand skills stacked together.
- **Against:** Seeking external approval from clients to feel valid. Neediness repels the clients you want most.
- **For:** Operating from a position of completeness — "a whole human, not a human with a hole that needs to be filled." Clients can feel the difference.
- **Against:** Abandoning skill acquisition in The Dip. Quitting at the plateau is the most common reason creatives stay underpaid.
- **For:** Charging more and working less as the recovery path from the burnout-resentment cycle. "Doubling your rates means you can cut your workload in half and make the same amount of money. This one act rebuilds your confidence and restores joy to your work."
- **For:** Reframing failure as tuition. "Failure is the tuition you pay for success."

## How This Applies as a Decision Lens

Use this framework when pricing any new engagement — especially when the instinct is to lower the number before the client even sees it. The diagnostic question is: "Am I pricing my cost, or am I pricing their outcome?" If you haven't yet asked the client what result they're trying to achieve and what that result is worth to their business, you don't have enough information to quote. The self-concept check is equally important: if a number feels uncomfortable to say out loud, that discomfort is data about your internal thermostat — not about what the market will bear. For Justin's AI services pivot specifically, this framework directly supports charging for business problem-solving (outcome) rather than hours of implementation work (cost), and it validates the instinct to qualify clients early on budget before investing time in diagnosis.
