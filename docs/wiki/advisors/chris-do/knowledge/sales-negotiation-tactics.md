# Sales Negotiation Tactics and the Diagnostic Conversation

**URL:** https://thefutur.com/watch/how-to-charge-for-creative-work-sales-masterclass
**Published:** 2024
**Type:** Video (55:49 min) -- Sales Masterclass, The Futur (negotiation and diagnostic tactics layer; pricing models covered separately in pricing-models-for-creative-work.md)

## Key Frameworks and Patterns

### The ICE Diagnostic Framework

Before discussing price or scope, Do runs a structured diagnostic conversation. The acronym ICE structures the discovery:

- **I for Impact:** "What impact will it have on the business if this problem is solved?" Forces the client to articulate value in their own words. Moves the conversation from cost to investment.
- **C for Challenge:** "What is getting in the way? What have you tried in the past? Why didn't it work?" Uncovers past failures and hidden obstacles. Reveals the true nature of the problem.
- **E for End-goal:** "What is the ultimate outcome you want?" Not what they want you to make, but the result they want to achieve. A client rarely wants a website -- they want to sell a product, create awareness, or generate leads. The website is one possible vehicle.

This is what Do calls being "diagnostic in your dialogue." You surface deeper issues before ever discussing a solution. The approach draws on Kevin T. Daley's Socratic Selling: no amount of pre-call research replaces the information gained by speaking directly to the decision-maker.

Key principle: "Don't start the party without the client." Ask open-ended questions that invite them to paint the full picture. Show up curious, not pre-baked.

### The Unselling Technique

In a live roleplay, Do demonstrates deliberate "unselling" -- actively challenging a client's assumed need to test their commitment and uncover the real problem.

A client believes he needs a new logo because his branding feels misaligned and cheap. Instead of scoping the project, Do dismantles the premise:

- "Do you think by changing the identity that that's going to do anything?"
- "In the real world do you buy products that you love from companies that are very valuable where their logo and identity system is trash?"

He points out Hilton, Marriott, and Norwegian Cruise Lines -- highly valuable companies with unremarkable logos. "The world is ugly. It's run by people who have no taste who hire people who have no taste to make things that are tasteless."

This is not an argument against good design. It is a logic test. Do forces the client to confront the reality that a new logo, in isolation, does not guarantee new customers. He is deliberately "unselling" the client's own request to surface the deeper business need underneath.

The unselling serves two purposes: (1) it positions you as a trusted advisor who puts the client's interest above your own revenue, and (2) it exposes misalignment between what the client says they want and what they actually need. Only after the real problem surfaces can you prescribe the right solution.

### The Logic Test of Commitment

After the client proposes a budget of "mid five figures" ($50K-$60K) for a brand identity, Do delivers the logic test:

"I'm going to do a ballpark estimation right now. On top of the 50 or $60,000... we're looking at another at least quarter million to $400,000."

This includes all touchpoints that must be redesigned for a cohesive premium experience: website, social media, video content, packaging, and more. A top-tier identity demands top-tier execution everywhere.

The client is shocked: "Way too much." The roleplay appears to break down. But this is the entire point.

Do has exposed a massive misalignment between the client's desire (a premium brand experience) and their willingness to invest in that outcome. This is information both parties need before wasting months of work. Better to surface it in a 30-minute conversation than after a $60K check clears.

### The Therapist Posture (Mindset of a Master)

The entire approach requires a fundamental mindset shift. Do operates from abundance, not lack. The reason he can "un-sell" a $60,000 project:

"You have to do what's right for the client. You may lose the business today, but you'll win the game tomorrow."

This is the posture of a therapist: listen, question, hold up a mirror so the client sees their own thoughts clearly. No agenda other than helping them find the truth.

Operating principle: **Assume everything that's said and presented to you is untrue.** Not cynicism -- critical thinking. "Clients are busy. They haven't formulated all their thoughts and theories on what they need to do... they ask for solutions they don't even know are connected to real results."

Your job is to help them connect the dots. You are an advocate for their business goals, not an advocate for your own services.

### Selling Time Back

The reframe that unlocks value-based conversations: you are not selling your time. You are selling people's time back to themselves.

"What you're doing is you're selling people's time back to themselves. The more valuable the person is, the more they'll pay you to buy back their time."

Time is the only non-renewable, perishable resource. You can always make more money. You can never make more time. High-value individuals understand this instinctively. Price against that understanding.

Do challenges the time-quality equivalency with an art market example: "Are those [Renaissance] paintings more valuable than a Picasso painting which seems like it doesn't take a whole lot of time to draw or to paint?" Value is in the idea, the novelty, the story, and the brand -- not the labor.

## Positions and Opinions

- Diagnose before prescribing. Always. No exceptions. The ICE framework provides structure.
- Deliberately unsell when a client's stated need does not match their actual problem. This builds trust and positions you as advisor, not vendor.
- Surface budget misalignment early through logic tests. Better to lose a deal in 30 minutes than deliver a mismatched result over 6 months.
- Operate from the therapist posture: professional skepticism, emotional detachment, genuine concern for the client's outcome.
- Assume what clients tell you is incomplete or misdiagnosed, not because they are dishonest but because they are busy and have not thought it through.
- Present 2-3 options with different pros, cons, and investment levels. Empower the client to choose, transforming the sales process from a pitch into a collaborative strategy session.

## How This Applies as a Decision Lens

When Justin enters sales conversations for the AI services pivot, the ICE framework provides an immediate structure: what is the business impact if this problem gets solved, what has gotten in the way, and what is the end-goal? The unselling technique is directly applicable when prospects ask for specific deliverables ("I need a chatbot" or "I need an automation") -- challenge the premise to surface the real business problem underneath. The logic test of commitment prevents scope misalignment: if a client wants premium AI-powered solutions but has a $2K budget, surface that gap immediately rather than trying to make it work. The therapist posture is the antidote to Justin's natural builder instinct to jump straight to solutions -- slow down, diagnose, let the client articulate the value themselves.
