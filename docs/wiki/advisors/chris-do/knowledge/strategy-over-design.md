# Strategy Over Aesthetics — How CORE Saved Blind and Repositioned Chris Do

**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yevZjd-YwUY
**Published:** 2018-11-09
**Type:** YouTube video (The Futur, 49:17)

## Key Frameworks and Patterns

### The Vendor-to-Consultant Shift

Chris Do's central diagnosis of why design businesses stagnate: clients treat designers as vendors — telling them what to do, how to do it, and how often — instead of consultants. The shift from vendor to consultant is the core career and business move. Aesthetics-first thinking keeps you in vendor mode. Strategy-first thinking earns you consultant status.

The distinction: vendors are told; consultants advise. You can only advise if you understand the business well enough to ask the right questions before picking up a pen.

### The CORE Framework

CORE is the brand strategy framework Chris Do learned and subsequently integrated into his company Blind's service offerings. It is a structured discovery process — not a design process — that runs before any visual or execution work begins. The framework organizes client work around:

- **Clarity** — getting clear on the business goals and challenges (not the aesthetic preferences)
- **Objectives** — aligning key stakeholders on measurable outcomes before execution begins
- **Research** — understanding the market, audience, and competitive landscape
- **Expression** — only then, translating strategy into visual and verbal identity

Note: The video describes CORE as a framework Chris learned through his friend Jose Caballo (a strategist), not one he invented himself. He then productized it as a core Blind offering.

### Discovery as a Separate, Billable Phase

A key structural move: discovery (the strategic phase) is separated from execution (design) and sold as its own deliverable. The discovery process includes:

- Facilitating stakeholder alignment (not just talking to one decision-maker)
- Clarifying goals and challenges before any creative brief is written
- Using worksheets and structured conversations to draw out business context
- Anticipating and preventing last-minute revisions by getting alignment upfront

The logic: last-minute client revisions happen because the wrong stakeholder approved work in isolation. Discovery surfaces those stakeholders and aligns them before execution starts — saving time and protecting margins.

### Aesthetics vs. Business Goals — The Reframe

Chris Do's diagnosis of most design business conversations: designers spend almost all their time talking about whether something looks good, feels fresh, or feels modern and innovative — and almost no time talking about the business goals or why the client is spending money in the first place.

The reframe: design is a medium for delivering a business outcome. The job is to understand the outcome first, then select the appropriate medium. This is what separates strategic designers from decorators.

### The Survival Story as Proof

Blind (Chris Do's motion design/branding firm) was in decline after the 2008 housing market collapse. Revenue was evaporating. They tried repositioning — adding web design, branding services — but none of it worked. Adding brand strategy to their offerings is what reversed the trajectory. The story functions as a proof point: strategy is not a luxury add-on; it's the business model.

## Positions and Opinions

- Designers who focus on aesthetics over business goals are positioned as vendors, not consultants — and will be treated accordingly.
- Repositioning by adding services (web, branding) without strategy underneath is surface-level and doesn't work.
- Brand strategy is not the client's job to bring to you — it's yours to facilitate.
- Discovery is a billable phase, not free pre-sales work. Clients pay to think through their own business with you.
- Stakeholder alignment is a deliverable, not a courtesy. If the wrong person approved the work, you will pay for it in revisions.
- The transition from "what does it look like" to "what does it need to accomplish" is the transition from junior to senior thinking.
- You cannot deliver across digital and other platforms effectively if you haven't understood the business context first.
- Most designers spend too much time in execution mode and almost no time in strategic framing — this is a learnable, correctable habit.

## How This Applies as a Decision Lens

Use this when evaluating whether a new client engagement is being scoped as a vendor job or a consultant engagement. If the conversation is dominated by deliverable lists, aesthetic preferences, or scope-of-work line items without any discussion of business outcomes, that's the vendor trap. The CORE lens asks: have you run a real discovery phase, surfaced the actual business goals, and aligned the decision-makers before any execution begins? For Justin, this maps directly to the BGD pivot — leading with "I solve business problems" rather than "I build websites" is exactly the vendor-to-consultant shift Chris Do is describing. The discovery phase is also a revenue model: charge for strategy separately, before any build work starts, and use it to qualify whether execution is even the right answer.
