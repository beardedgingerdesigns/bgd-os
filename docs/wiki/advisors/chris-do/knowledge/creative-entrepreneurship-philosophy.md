# Value-Based Pricing and the Business of Creative Work

**URL:** https://creativechair.org/chris-do/
**Published:** August 2017
**Type:** Interview article

## Key Frameworks and Patterns

### Price the Client, Not the Job

The foundational framework Chris Do is known for. The deliverable (a logo, an identity system) has no inherent price. What you charge is a function of what the work is worth to *that specific client at that specific moment* — not hours spent, not complexity, not your costs. The floor is what you need; the ceiling doesn't exist.

He walked this into practice gradually: his public workshops started with a $15K-$30K logo range because jumping straight to six figures would have broken the audience's frame of reference. By the time of this interview he had submitted a $150K logo and was closing multiple clients above $100K.

### Risk-as-Pricing-Signal

The mechanism underneath "price the client" is client exposure to risk. The question to ask is: *how much does it cost them if you get this wrong?* A startup redesigning a logo risks little. A company with $500M in revenue risks millions in lost customer recognition, opportunity cost, and brand equity. The same creative output carries a completely different price tag because the downside is categorically different.

He uses Nike and Apple as anchors: their values and mission may be the same as when they started, but their operations — and therefore their risk exposure on design decisions — are enormous. A bad design decision at that scale can cost them "millions in lost revenue and billions of dollars in opportunity cost."

### The Futur's 4-Step Education Ladder

Chris Do's education platform (The Futur, formerly The Skool) operates on a staged-expansion model:

1. **Credibility** — become the number one resource for design education online
2. **Integration** — embed into existing brick-and-mortar university curricula
3. **Own institution** — build a 50,000 sq. ft. state-of-the-art learning center
4. **Global expansion** — replicate the physical experience internationally

The content covers both creative soft skills (design thinking, storytelling, typography, process) and "B-school concepts" — the business and marketing literacy that design schools historically skip.

### GBO (Global Business Objective) Thinking

Chris Do uses the term GBO to describe a company's top-line strategic focus. At the time of this interview, The Futur's GBOs were: grow YouTube to 200K subscribers and grow revenue month over month. This is a useful framing device — not goals, not KPIs, but the singular thing the whole machine is pointed at.

## Positions and Opinions

- Designers chronically undercharge because they anchor on their own costs (time, effort) rather than on the client's upside or downside
- There is no ethical problem with charging different clients different amounts for the same output — value is contextual, not universal
- "Value isn't determined by you, it's determined by the client" — the market, not the maker, sets price
- The gap between design school and business reality is the core problem The Futur exists to solve; traditional design education teaches craft but not commerce
- Creatives need to be comfortable talking about money at the level of business impact, not task completion
- The incumbent design education establishment ("sleeping giants") will only respond to pressure from a credible alternative; the strategy is to become that alternative before asking for partnership

## How This Applies as a Decision Lens

When pricing any engagement, the question is not "how long will this take" or "what have I charged for this before" — it is "what is on the line for this client if I get it wrong, and what upside does success unlock for them." This reframe is directly applicable to Justin's AI pivot: BGD's services are not website builds, they are operational solutions. The client buying a $750/mo automation is not buying hours of work; they are buying relief from a pain that costs them time, revenue, or competitive position. Pricing conversations should start with diagnosing the client's risk exposure and business objective, then working backward to a number that reflects a fraction of the value created — not a markup on effort expended. Chris Do's framework also supports tiered pricing (as Jon Liebl would sell it) because different clients face different stakes for the same category of solution.
