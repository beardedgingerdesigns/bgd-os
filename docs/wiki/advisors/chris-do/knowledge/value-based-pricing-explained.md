# Value-Based Pricing: Price the Customer, Not the Job

**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv46Hf8aNyU
**Published:** 2024-07-26
**Type:** YouTube video (3:17 clip from THE 505 PODCAST)

## Key Frameworks and Patterns

### Time-Based Pricing Is Self-Centered

Chris Do's core critique of hourly/time-based pricing: it is entirely about the provider, not the client. "My time is worth a certain amount of money, and based on units of time I'm going to multiply my value times time and sell it to you" — this, he argues, takes no account of what matters to the buyer. He calls it a "very self-centered approach to pricing."

### The Same Logo Has Three Different Prices

His central illustration: a logo delivered to a startup, an established company, and a Fortune 100 company carries vastly different value to each. A flat $10,000 quote lands wrong for all three — too high for the startup, acceptable but tight for the mid-market company, and suspiciously cheap (disqualifying) for the enterprise. The takeaway: a fixed rate optimized for one buyer is wrong for everyone else.

### Price the Customer, Not the Job

The explicit framework: "Value-based pricing says you price the customer and not the job." Each client has a problem to solve relative to their own context, stakes, and perception of value. The work product is identical; the pricing conversation is individual.

### Value Is Relative, Subjective, and Contextual

Chris Do uses Jonathan Stark's example of a kid spending real money on cosmetic video game upgrades (coins for a suit that performs no better, just looks different) to illustrate that value has nothing to do with intrinsic utility. The kid values it; the father sees zero value. Same asset, opposite perception. He parallels this to Pokemon cards — a million-dollar ultra-rare card is worthless to someone who doesn't collect them, priceless to a collector. Conclusion: value is individual, contextual, and varies greatly.

### The Discovery Question: What Is a Logo Worth to You?

Before pricing, he advocates asking whether solving the problem actually moves the client closer to their goals. If the answer is "not really, my business is doing fine," he doesn't want to sell the logo. But if the client prints $35 million/year and a logo error cascades into lost revenue, that logo is suddenly a high-stakes, high-value engagement. The Tropicana redesign case study anchors this: a better-looking design cost millions in revenue because customers couldn't find the product on shelves — they had to revert. That's where design actually matters, and that's where pricing should reflect the stakes.

## Positions and Opinions

- Advocates for: asking discovery questions that surface what the outcome is worth to the specific client before naming a number.
- Advocates for: treating value as subjective and individual, not a fixed market rate.
- Argues against: hourly billing as a pricing model — it structurally ignores the client's context.
- Argues against: a one-size quote that lands equally wrong across different-sized clients.
- Implicit position: designers and service professionals undercharge enterprise clients while overcharging small ones — both failures stem from the same flat-rate thinking.
- Cites Jonathan Stark as an influence/source on value subjectivity.
- The Kia logo redesign and the Tropicana redesign are his go-to real-world cases showing that design decisions carry financial consequences orders of magnitude beyond the design fee.

## How This Applies as a Decision Lens

When pricing any BGD engagement, this framework pushes against quoting a standard rate and toward opening with discovery: what problem does this solve, and what is not solving it costing the client? A $5,000 AI automation that saves a mid-market company $80K/year in labor is priced wrong at $5,000 — and priced wrong again if quoted the same way to a small business where the savings are $8K. Chris Do's lens says the conversation about price should come after the conversation about stakes, and the number should be anchored to the client's outcome, not the provider's time.
