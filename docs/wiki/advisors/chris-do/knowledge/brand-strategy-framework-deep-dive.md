# Brand Strategy as a Client Deliverable -- The CORE Framework in Practice

**URL:** https://thefutur.com/watch/how-to-start-brand-strategy-for-designers
**Published:** 2024
**Type:** Video -- Brand Strategy for Designers (Part 1), The Futur
**Also draws from:** https://thefutur.com/content/charging-5000-for-strategy (Melinda Livsey case study)

## Key Frameworks and Patterns

### The CORE Framework Origin and Philosophy

The CORE framework was co-developed by Jose Caballer from the Silicon Valley UX/startup world. It positions brand work at the intersection of three circles: the brand, the user, and the business goals.

Do's critical adaptation: he reordered the starting point. Caballer typically started with brand voice, tone, and culture. Do insisted on starting with the user first. "Shouldn't we be focused on the user first?" This was not a minor shuffle -- it was a philosophical shift.

The reasoning: "It is much easier for a brand to understand the needs of its users and create products and services for them. It is much harder to have a pre-existing product and then go searching for users to buy it."

Starting with the user changes every downstream decision. It transforms design from self-expression into service.

### The User Profile Revolution

Before CORE, Do spent nearly two decades designing without user profiles. "I didn't understand that we're designing for someone." This is the critical blind spot for aesthetics-first designers.

Caballer introduced the concept of defining, with granular detail, who will be using the product. You design different experiences for end-users, administrators, content creators, partners. When you design for a specific someone, it fundamentally shapes every decision.

This was the antidote to what Do calls "digital brochures" -- beautifully designed but ultimately useless websites. With a user-centric approach, features and functions had purpose.

### Brand Strategy as Separate, Billable Phase

The Melinda Livsey case study demonstrates the transition from free-strategy-bundled-with-design to strategy-as-standalone-product:

- Before: Melinda used a questionnaire as her "strategy" and charged $0 for it. Top branding package: $6,300 all-in.
- After (6 months of coaching with Do): Strategy separated out at $5,000 as a standalone deliverable. Logo design at $5,000+ on top.
- Key shift: "I'm not really selling. I'm trying to align with my client... I am offering them strategy first, really as the only option if they would like to work with me."

Do's coaching principle: "You're not an order taker. You're an advisor to the client." Separating strategy validates that thinking has standalone value. When you lump it in, you signal it is worthless.

### The In-Person Workshop Model

Strategy delivery through facilitated workshops with executive stakeholders. The Tango case study (IT expense management company) illustrates the process:

1. Full-day workshop. Executives flew in from across the country.
2. Do ran CORE exercises, facilitating discovery rather than presenting conclusions.
3. Hours of executive jargon. Do's mind was "about to explode" -- couldn't understand the acronyms. He had to choose: pretend to get it, or admit ignorance.
4. He chose vulnerability: "Hey everybody, you guys are talking inside baseball. I do not understand what you're talking about. You need to explain this to me like I'm your mom or a fifth grader."
5. The tension broke. Executives laughed with relief. Do had released a pressure valve they didn't know was sealed.

Key lesson: **Your bravery in admitting what you don't know is a tool to force clarity.** If the strategy consultant does not understand the business, the marketing will not communicate it either.

### Two Strategic Insights From Discovery

From the Tango workshop, two pillars emerged that drove every creative decision:

1. **Saving money.** The Chief Revenue Officer distilled their complex service: "Chris, I don't know, man. What we do is not sexy, but what we do is we save people money." This was the first strategic pillar.

2. **The IT Superhero.** A disturbing attitude surfaced: executives spoke of their IT manager clients with disdain, calling them a "necessary evil." Do reframed: "They're not just the hero of every operation. They're the superhero." Disdain was replaced by empathy and gratitude. This became the more powerful second pillar.

### From Insight to Execution: Strategy Informing Design

The two pillars directly informed every creative decision:

- Logo: the descender of the letter 'g' was cut off to create a sharp line. In presentation, Do told them: "You guys are about the bottom line." The clients immediately got it -- the logo was a symbol of their business promise.
- Copywriting built on both pillars: "Spend less, do more." "Gain visibility of shadow IT." "With great visibility comes great power." "The diligent dollar defender." "Titan of IT."
- Result: what started as a sub-$100K strategy and identity project grew to a $250K relationship, including a $100K video.

The personal result: "Chris, you've been able to do more in four hours than we have been able to do in months." Executives gave Do a hug. "I've been working in commercial production, working with ad agencies for years at this point. None of them ever gave me a hug. It's because they saw me as a person who made things, not as a partner trying to help them solve a big problem."

### Deconstructing Brand Vocabulary

Do insists on precise definitions:

- **Brand:** "A person's gut feeling about a product, service, or organization" (Marty Neumeier). Jeff Bezos version: "Your brand is what people say about you when you're not in the room." It is your reputation.
- **Branding:** The deliberate act of trying to shape and influence that perception. The sum of every interaction a person has with a company.
- **Logo:** "A logo is just a container for meaning." An empty bucket. Everyone in the company is responsible for pouring meaning into it. The swoosh holds decades of Nike's athlete endorsements, campaigns, innovation, and retail experiences. The designer's job is to create a beautiful container. The strategist's job is to influence what goes inside.

"Stop being a decorator. Become an architect."

## Positions and Opinions

- Strategy must precede execution. "If we actually start with the logo first, we're going to have a very shell of a brand, and it's not going to be held up by anything because there's no goals, and there's no purpose."
- Separate strategy as a billable phase. Bundling it for free signals it has no value. Start at $5,000 and scale to $15,000+.
- Start with the user, not the brand. The brand adapts to serve user needs, not the other way around.
- Admit what you don't understand during workshops. Forced clarity is the strategist's superpower. If you can't understand it, the customer cannot either.
- A brand can never be fake for long. Branding is not cosmetic -- every interaction either reinforces or undermines the brand promise.
- Refuse to do design-only engagements. If the client won't do strategy, refer them elsewhere. "I feel that I've actually used to do this, I used to be a freelancer... I ended up being an expense. And I don't want to do that to my clients anymore."

## How This Applies as a Decision Lens

The CORE framework validates Justin's pivot from "I build websites" to "I solve business problems." Every BGD engagement should start with discovery (user research, business goals, strategic pillars) before jumping to deliverables. The Tango case study is a direct template for how AI services engagements should run: facilitated workshop, admit ignorance about the client's domain, force clarity through questions, derive strategic pillars, then execute against them. The strategy-as-separate-phase principle applies directly: Justin should charge for AI audit and strategy before any implementation work. The Melinda Livsey trajectory ($0 to $5K for strategy in 6 months) is a benchmark for what the AI services tier can achieve when the thinking is valued independently of the building.
