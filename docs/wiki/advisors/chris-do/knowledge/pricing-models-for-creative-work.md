# Value-Based Pricing and the Sales Mindset for Creative Work

**URL:** https://thefutur.com/watch/how-to-charge-for-creative-work-sales-masterclass
**Published:** 2024-01-04
**Type:** Video (55:49 min) — Full Sales Masterclass and Role-Play, published by The Futur

## Key Frameworks and Patterns

### The Three Pricing Models

Chris Do maps out three pricing frameworks creatives use. His position is not that value-based pricing wins every time — it's that you should use the right model for the task.

1. **Time-based pricing** — charging for your hours. Transparent but commoditizing. Caps your income at hours available.
2. **Project-based pricing** — calculate internal cost, add a profit buffer, quote the total. More stable but still cost-anchored.
3. **Value-based pricing** — price based on the value created for the client, not the cost to produce it. The hardest to execute, but the most fair and the highest ceiling.

His direct quote on why value-based is hardest: "Value based pricing is the most difficult one to understand and to implement... because you have to have a business dialogue with your customer. Each customer is different. They value things differently."

And why it's also the fairest: "It's also, coincidentally, ironically, the most fair pricing model because it could mean that you're going to price something a lot more and something a lot less — because the pendulum swings both ways."

### The Self-Undermining Cycle (The Creative's Guilt Problem)

Creatives chronically undercharge because of a psychological trap specific to doing work they love. The pattern:

1. You think of a number — say, $5,000.
2. Self-doubt kicks in: "That sounds like a lot. This is easy for me. Why not $2,500?"
3. You present the lower number. The client negotiates. You accept $2,000.
4. Resentment builds. You cut corners. Work quality drops. Client is underwhelmed.
5. The cycle repeats at a lower baseline.

Chris Do's framing: "This bitterness poisons the work. You cut corners, rush the process, and deliver something that is merely 'good enough.'"

Root cause: "I would feel a certain amount of embarrassment and guilt for someone to pay me to do that — because I actively and happily pay to be able to do this."

### Stop Talking About Yourself — The Conversation Reframe

The fix is shifting from a cost-based internal monologue to an externally-focused, value-discovery conversation. Pricing based on your time, effort, or experience is "a self-centered, egocentric approach." The client only cares about what you can do for them.

The questions to ask instead of calculating hours:

- "What are you trying to achieve by doing this?"
- "Why are you doing this now?"
- "What will this do for your business?"

When a client says a new website could generate $10,000/month more revenue, the conversation is no longer about a $2,500 logo. It's anchored to $120,000/year in upside. You have reframed from your cost to their gain.

### Diagnose Before Prescribing

Creatives make the critical error of jumping straight to deliverables — selling graphic design, layouts, and project management instead of business outcomes. This commoditizes the work and forces price competition. The doctor analogy is implicit: you don't prescribe before you diagnose. Ask questions until you understand the business problem, then position your work as the solution.

"The problem is that most creatives are so eager to jump to solutions, to talk about deliverables, that they never properly diagnose the problem. They sell a list of activities instead of a business outcome."

### Client Filtering and Non-Negotiables

Before any pricing conversation, filter for fit. Define your deal-killers:

- **Industry filters** — refuse categories you won't work with (tobacco, alcohol, etc.)
- **Behavior filters** — red flag: "I'll know it when I see it." That phrase signals indecisiveness and unlimited revisions. Exit professionally: "After thinking about this, I don't think I'm a good fit for you."
- **Budget filter** — address money upfront. Don't waste time with bargain shoppers. Example: "I need to know: do you have at least $X for this project? If not, we're not going to be a good fit."

This is directly aligned with Blair Enns' concept of a minimum level of engagement.

### Emotional Detachment in Negotiation

A client pushing back on price is not a personal attack. It is a business tactic. Reacting emotionally is a sign of professional immaturity.

"Reacting emotionally is a sign of professional immaturity and poor business skills. It's two people coming together to discuss budgets and one of us determining if it's right or wrong."

The car dealership model: a salesperson doesn't apologize for the sticker price, justify it, rationalize it, or try to convince you. The price is the price.

The mental precondition for holding the line: "If you do not believe that you can create more value than what you charge — if it's true — work on acquiring more skills." Confidence in your rate comes from genuine confidence in your output relative to what you charge.

### The Belief System Prerequisite

All pricing frameworks fail without the right internal foundation. What you believe you are worth determines what you get. Chris Do's language: "This is the invisible force governing your income, your relationships, and ultimately, your worth in a business context. What you think you are determines what you get."

### Price the Client, Not the Job

From a 2017 interview corroborating the masterclass: "Don't charge by the hour. Price the client, not the job." Two clients requesting the same logo get different prices because they derive different value from it.

Logo pricing example: "I charge a flat rate for logo design. That rate is between $15,000–$30,000 and will be based on how much the logo is worth to them." He later closed logo work at $150,000.

## Positions and Opinions

- **For:** Value-based pricing as the primary framework for strategic/creative work where business outcomes are identifiable.
- **For:** Using hourly or project-based pricing when scope is genuinely unclear or the work is more transactional — "use the right pricing methodology for the task."
- **For:** Asking business-outcome questions before any pricing discussion.
- **For:** Filtering clients on budget upfront to protect your time and energy.
- **For:** Emotional detachment in negotiation — it is a business process, not a personal evaluation.
- **Against:** Charging by the hour as a default for creative work. It caps income and commoditizes the work.
- **Against:** Selling deliverables (layouts, design files, hours) instead of outcomes.
- **Against:** Pre-negotiating against yourself before the client even sees a number.
- **Against:** Aggressive outbound prospecting as a primary strategy — it signals desperation and degrades positioning. Recommends 20% outbound / 80% inbound for those who need to fill the pipeline.

## How This Applies as a Decision Lens

When advising Justin on any service pricing or client sales conversation — particularly the AI services pivot — this framework pushes against quoting time, effort, or deliverables. The first question is always: what is this worth to the client's business? If a client's problem is costing them $50K/year in lost time or missed revenue, the engagement should be priced against that outcome, not against hours. The creative guilt trap maps directly onto Justin's situation: technical work that feels "easy" after building competency still has high business value, and undercharging to get the "easy yes" sets the wrong floor. The emotional detachment principle is equally relevant — in negotiation with Jon Liebl's clients or existing BGD clients, pushback on price is a business conversation, not a verdict on the work's quality. Define non-negotiables and budget minimums before the sales conversation starts.
