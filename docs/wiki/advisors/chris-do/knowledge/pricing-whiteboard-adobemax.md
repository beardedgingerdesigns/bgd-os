# Pricing Intangible Creative Work: Value, Confidence, and the Sales Conversation

**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6rZQPhXGOlk
**Published:** 2023-11-26 (Adobe MAX 2023 conference talk)
**Type:** YouTube video / conference talk (1h 13m, The Futur channel)

## Key Frameworks and Patterns

### The Core Problem: Quantifying the Intangible

Chris opens by naming the root of why creatives undercharge: they operate in the realm of art, style, class, taste, and brand -- all intangible -- while clients think in a quantifiable universe. "If we can't quantify it, it becomes very difficult to communicate to someone who thinks in a quantifiable universe." The talk exists to give creatives actionable tools to bridge that gap in the next client conversation.

### Independent Business Owner vs. Freelancer

Chris insists on a language shift: stop calling yourself a freelancer. "A freelancer is a person who sells their time for money, works in somebody else's rules." The reframe matters because identity shapes behavior. If you think of yourself as an independent business owner, you negotiate differently, set your own rules, and think about demand and capacity rather than hours traded.

### Beam of Light: Specialize to Be Remembered

When people list every service they offer ("graphic design, layout, project management, and..."), Chris calls this "selling rainbows." The antidote is narrowing to a single focused offering. "The more complicated you make something sound, the harder it is for somebody to remember it, tell someone else." Simplified positioning powers word-of-mouth (WMA -- Word of Mouth Advertising), which he identifies as the primary lead generation engine for most creatives.

### The Confidence Equation (David C. Baker concept)

Confidence = Opportunities / Capacity. When you have one opportunity and can handle three projects, your confidence is low. When you have 35 opportunities and can only take three, your confidence score is ~10x. "The goal in running any business is to have more opportunities than capacity." The practical move is to drive up opportunities (marketing, positioning) while protecting capacity (saying no, being selective).

### Manufactured Scarcity

Scarcity communicates value before price is ever discussed. Two real-world examples he uses: luxury real estate that hides the price (if you have to ask, you can't afford it), and art museums where work is not for sale. Being discriminating in who you work with signals scarcity: "The more discriminating you are, you're communicating scarcity." Turning away a prospect who isn't a fit isn't losing -- it's messaging.

### The Sales Conversation Map (Whiteboard Process)

Chris draws out the typical sales process and identifies where it breaks down. The correct sequence:

1. **WMA / inbound lead** -- word of mouth, referral, social
2. **30-minute fit check** -- not a pitch, a filter. Ask: "Do you fit here? Do I want to work with you?" Be willing to say: "You deserve someone who's going to be excited and passionate about what you do. I'm just not that person."
3. **Discovery / understanding their problem** -- not a logistics intake. Ask: "Why are we having this conversation today? What problem are you trying to solve in your business? How would solving this improve your situation?"
4. **Second call: recap + offer** -- "This is what you said you want. Here's what I can offer you." Then logistics. Then price. Price comes after value is established, not before.

The common failure mode: asking for price too early, before the client understands the value being delivered.

### Intentional Listening and Mirroring Language

This is the central tactical framework. "Intentional listening" means not just hearing but feeding back exact language the client uses. The rule: **do not introduce new words**. If a client says "how do I measure that?" -- do not substitute "goals." Say: "I don't know -- how will *you* measure that?" Mirror their words back. This accomplishes two things: it shows the client they are heard, and it forces the client to articulate their own success metrics, which then become the basis for pricing value.

### Success Metrics as Pricing Anchor

When the client says "we'll sell more units" or "we want to look less outdated" -- that is the value anchor, not your time. The question to reach it: "How do you measure success?" When the client can't answer that question, Chris teaches to reflect it back: "I don't know -- how will you measure that?" The client defines the value; the creative prices relative to it. "How valuable is this to them? It depends on what their success metric is."

### Sell the Dream, Not the Making

"I sell the conversation. I sell the dream. I sell the potential. I sell my listening skills. The making of it is the easiest part. What are we making, and why are we making it -- that's the hard part. That's what they'll pay you for." In the context of logos, Chris argues you are not selling a mark -- you are closing the imagination gap between what a logo is and what it could become in application. Use mockups, photography, art direction. "You're selling the romance of the logo, not the actual making of it."

### The 50-Year CEO Signal

When a prospect mentions legacy, tenure, or competitive context ("we look outdated after 50 years"), that is a trigger to mentally reprice upward. Established companies have brand equity to protect, have worked with agencies before, and have higher revenue at stake. "In my mind already, I'm on my jack-up-the-price rate."

## Positions and Opinions

- Avoid the word "freelancer" -- it anchors you to a time-for-money mental model that caps your earning and confidence.
- Never lead with price. Price follows demonstrated value and articulated success metrics.
- Asking "what's your budget?" early is a mistake. Budget conversations belong after value is established.
- Being picky about clients is not arrogance -- it is a business strategy that communicates scarcity and raises perceived value.
- "We mistakenly believe that just because somebody comes calling, we got to do their business. We think we have to close this client. And it's not the case."
- The creative's discomfort with money is the root cause of undercharging, not lack of talent or market conditions.
- KPI / "key performance indicator" language alienates clients. Translate: "How do you measure success?" or "How will you know when we've solved the problem?"
- Presenting a logo on a white background grid is selling yourself short. Put it on real things. Close the imagination gap.
- AI tools (text-to-vector) threaten the "making" part of design work, not the strategic and communicative parts. The value has always been in the conversation, not the craft execution.

## How This Applies as a Decision Lens

Chris Do's framework reframes every pricing conversation as a value conversation first. The lever is not what you charge but what you have learned to ask and listen for. Applied to BGD's AI services pivot: before quoting a tier or an hourly rate, run the fit check, ask "what problem are you trying to solve in your business," and mirror back the client's own language about what success looks like. The client who says "we're losing bids because our proposals take three days" has just handed you the anchor for pricing an AI automation at 10x the cost-of-time calculation -- because you know the value outcome. The talk also validates Justin's instinct to be selective and premium-positioned: manufactured scarcity via a short client roster and a visible body of work creates the confidence score that lets you quote without flinching.
