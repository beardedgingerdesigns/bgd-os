# Branding and Positioning Strategy for Non-Creatives

**URL:** https://thefutur.com/watch/branding-for-non-creatives-keynote
**Published:** 2024-01-10
**Type:** Keynote/video (1h 13m) — originally presented at the "Creative Business Models for Accountants" conference

## Key Frameworks and Patterns

### Framework 1: Brand = Gut Feeling (Marty Neumeier's Definition)

Do opens by dismantling the most common misconception: that branding is about logos, color palettes, and taglines. He borrows from brand strategist Marty Neumeier (*The Brand Gap*):

**"A brand is a person's gut feeling about a product, service, or organization."**

The power shifts from producer to perceiver. The logo is just a container — a symbol that collects meaning. You can't control the gut feeling. You can only influence it.

Key implication: "It's not what you say it is — it's what they say it is." If your team is curt and your policies nickel-and-dime clients, that's your brand, regardless of what your About page claims.

### Framework 2: Branding = Impression Management

Since you can't control feelings, the practice of branding is impression management — a concept Do attributes to Dr. Christine Lussier.

Every interaction manages an impression: how you dress, the words you choose, your energy, the consistency of your behavior. The job interview is the reference case: you dress for the job you want, prepare your answers, present the best version of yourself. That's impression management done intentionally.

Bezos's formulation reinforces this: *"Your brand is what people say about you when you're not in the room."*

**The authenticity line:** When the projected persona drifts too far from the true self, it becomes inauthentic. Do is explicit: people are "very good at sniffing out inauthentic brands." The goal is to be the best, most consistent version of your true self — not to invent a character.

**The Brand Iceberg:** Visual identity (logo, colors, typefaces) is above the waterline. Below it: beliefs and values, and the customer experience. Authentic branding aligns all layers.

### Framework 3: Positioning = Hearts, Minds, and Competitors

Do's definition: **Positioning is the space that you occupy in the hearts and minds of your customers relative to your competitors.**

The phrase "relative to your competitors" is load-bearing. Positioning is never absolute — it only exists in contrast. He cites Al Ries and Jack Trout (*Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind*) as the canonical source.

The canvas analogy: a canvas with paint spilled on it is worthless. Put it in a gallery with a velvet rope and it's art. The object didn't change — the frame did. Positioning is the frame.

### Framework 4: Guerrilla Positioning — Reframe Their Strength as a Weakness

Effective positioning is picking a fight. The tactic: take your competitor's greatest strength and, through reframing, turn it into a weakness. Three classic examples Do uses:

- **Listerine vs. Scope:** Listerine built its brand on medicinal power — it killed germs. Scope entered and reframed that strength as a weakness: Listerine gives you "medicine breath." Scope offered fresh breath. Listerine's clinical efficacy became a liability.
- **Avis vs. Hertz:** Hertz was the dominant #1 rental car company. Avis reframed being #2 as a virtue: "We try harder." They turned their underdog position into a service promise.
- **PC vs. Mac (Apple's "Get a Mac" campaign):** PC held 97% of the market in 2006. Apple never talked specs. The campaign simply repositioned the dominant player as old, bloated, uncool, and out of date — and did it so effectively it infuriated Bill Gates. Do's read: "You know the advertising is working when the CEO is upset."

The principle: *It's not about being better. It's about being different in a way that matters to the customer.*

### Framework 5: The Anti-Competitive Playbook (3-Step)

The purpose of strong positioning is to reduce or outright eliminate competition. Do's three-step process:

1. **Identify who you're actually competing against.** Not aspirationally — realistically. Who else is in the running when a client is considering you?
2. **Ask what that competition is known for.** Name their top three perceived traits. (A massive accounting firm might be: big, faceless, impersonal.)
3. **Be the anti of whatever they are.** If they're a big faceless organization, you're the person who cares about their business and stays up late worrying if they'll make payroll. You reframe their scale as a weakness and your size as a strength.

When you're clear about what you stand for and what you stand against, you attract the right people and repel the wrong ones. You stop being a commodity and start being a category of one.

### Framework 6: Brand Language — The Tribal Vocabulary

Powerful brands create their own vocabulary — a "secret language" that signals membership. Do uses an unconventional reference: the Heaven's Gate cult systematically redefined common words (members didn't have "bodies"; they had "vessels") to create in-group identity. Brands do the same: Nike's "Air" technology, Adidas's "Adiprene" foam. Proprietary terms make customers feel part of a tribe.

The Harley-Davidson "Roll Your Own" campaign illustrates this: a donut becomes a "Morning Donut" with the copy "Raise hell before lunch." Everyday objects reframed in brand language. The visual identity follows: distressed, raw, rugged, unpolished, rebellious, masculine.

### Framework 7: The Premium Pricing Signal

When positioning eliminates competition, premium pricing becomes possible and necessary. Do's argument:

- **Price communicates position.** A high price signals value. He illustrates with a personal story: he once listed a speaking fee he thought was absurd to discourage a booking, and someone paid it. That moment reframed what he thought he was worth.
- **A high price acts as a filter.** It repels people who aren't serious believers in your value and attracts people who are invested.
- **A premium buyer is a different buyer.** They're more likely to follow your advice, less likely to question your process. They see you as an expert, not a vendor.
- **The Friday Night Phone Call test.** If a client calls at 6pm on a Friday, are you annoyed or happy to pick up? *"If you charge enough, I pick up the phone. How you doin'? What do you need?"* Premium pricing allows you to serve without resentment, focusing on outcomes instead of counting hours.

### Framework 8: Marketing vs. Advertising (Seth Godin's Distinction)

Do draws a hard line: marketing is not advertising. Advertising is intrusive and repetitive — messages forced on people who don't want them. He adopts Seth Godin's definition:

**Marketing is the generous act of helping people get what they want.**

Godin's operating principle: *"Do something that matters for people who care."* Find a passionate audience and serve them, rather than interrupting the masses.

This leads to **Permission Marketing** — customers voluntarily participating in an ongoing campaign of increasing value. Three tests for permission marketing:
- Is it **anticipated**? Are people looking forward to hearing from you?
- Is it **personal**? Does it feel made for them?
- Is it **relevant**? Does it address their specific needs?

The customer journey model Do uses: Awareness → Interest → Desire → Conversion → **Advocacy**. Most businesses stop at conversion. The real leverage is advocacy — creating an experience so good that customers become your volunteer sales force.

## Positions and Opinions

- Strongly against confusing brand with visual identity. The logo is a container, not the brand itself.
- Against trying to control brand perception — you can only influence it.
- Against competing on price. Price is a signal of position; racing to the bottom destroys the brand.
- Against advertising as a substitute for marketing. Interruption is a tax; permission is an investment.
- Against inauthenticity. Fake brands are sniffed out. The projected persona must be rooted in the actual self.
- For positioning as a proactive, even aggressive, strategic act — "guerrilla marketing."
- For premium pricing as a client-quality filter, not just a revenue mechanism.
- For building a brand vocabulary and tribal identity that makes customers feel like insiders.
- For advocacy as the end goal of the marketing funnel — not conversion.

## How This Applies as a Decision Lens

When evaluating any business positioning or offer design decision, Do's framework asks: are you being perceived the way you intend, by the people you want to serve, relative to the alternatives they're considering? The lens cuts through vanity decisions (logo refreshes, tagline rewrites) and pushes toward the harder questions — who is the actual competition, what are they known for, and how do you reframe their strength as your differentiation? For BGD's pivot to "I solve business problems with AI," this framework is directly applicable: the competition isn't other AI consultants (there aren't many credible ones in the Des Moines mid-market), it's the perception that this kind of help is out of reach or requires a big agency. The anti-competitive play is to reframe the agency's scale as bureaucracy and Justin's operator model as speed, access, and plain-language translation.
