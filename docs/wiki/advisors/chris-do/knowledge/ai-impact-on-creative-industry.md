# AI's Impact on the Creative Industry -- Taste, Judgment, and the Great Divide

**URL:** https://thefutur.com/watch/future-of-ai-in-creative-industry
**Published:** 2025
**Type:** Video/Podcast -- The Future of AI for Creatives (Chris Do and Kevin Lau), The Futur

## Key Frameworks and Patterns

### The Great Divide

There is a fault line running through the creative industry, and it is not between humans and machines. It is between two types of creatives:

- **Seasoned professionals** -- directors and leaders who have built careers orchestrating complex projects. AI is a powerful new tool for them.
- **Aspiring artists** -- those still fighting to make a living. AI feels like an existential threat.

"The people I know who are professionals who are creative who know how to direct and lead teams and put things together, they're not as threatened by AI as the people who have yet to kind of make their living doing it." -- Kevin Lau

This is not a story about robots taking jobs. This is a story about where true creative value lies in an age of infinite content. The answer is not in the execution, but in the judgment.

### Taste as Currency

In a world of infinite execution capability, discernment becomes the scarcest resource. The ability to know what is good, what is right for the project, and what will connect with an audience is the ultimate human advantage.

Do references Pharrell Williams on Justin Timberlake's first solo album: "This is going to be hot. This guy's got really good taste." People like Pharrell or Rick Rubin are synthesizers -- they pull from a vast library of influences across music, art, and culture to create something new. They are not technicians; they are curators of ideas.

"I think one of the biggest skills that you're going to need to have is a broad understanding of art, music, literature, history." Without this foundation, you are at the mercy of the machine's suggestions.

### Taste Is Not Innate -- It Is Built

Do argues passionately that taste is a skill, not a gift. Personal story: "Two of my favorite colors used to be purple and teal. I grew up in the valley... I wasn't exposed to art, architecture, photography, fine art, none of that stuff."

His transformation came at ArtCenter College of Design -- exposure to masters, forced engagement with craftsmanship. He learned the difficulty of creating a perfect edge, the nuance of materials, principles of composition. His taste was not born; it was built through exposure, education, and practice.

### A Curriculum for the AI Age

Do outlines what creatives need to develop to remain valuable when AI handles execution:

- **Deep History.** You must know what came before to create what comes next. Understanding Dadaism vs. Constructivism, Brutalism vs. Modernism is not academic -- it is practical. It allows you to direct an AI with precision: "I think it's a little bit of a Matisse but in the early works with a little bit of like a dadaist kind of approach." This makes you the director, not the operator.
- **Rhetoric and Critical Thinking.** How to speak, think, and build arguments. The ability to articulate why something works or doesn't work.
- **Visual Communication.** Beyond decorating -- using aesthetics to amplify a message.
- **Critical Dialogue and Analysis.** Deconstruct a piece of art, music, or film. Why does it work? What are the subcomponents? How are they arranged? This analytical skill reveals the mechanics of good taste.
- **Craftsmanship.** Even if you never physically build another thing, understanding the difficulty of making something well imprints appreciation for quality.

### The Historical Parallel -- 1990s Motion Design Revolution

Do and Lau both came up in the 1990s Los Angeles motion design scene. The parallels to today's AI moment are direct:

- **New tools** (After Effects, Flash) put powerful capabilities into designer hands for the first time.
- **New talent** (art school kids with design sensibility) brought sophisticated aesthetics to screens previously owned by expensive post-production operators.
- **New demand** (ad agencies hungry for new looks) bypassed rigid traditional production houses.

From the creative chaos of that era, an entire new industry was born. "The rules weren't even written so you can't even say breaking the rules. We're just trying stuff and seeing what we can get away with."

The lesson: every new tool wave creates temporary disruption and permanent opportunity for those who lead with taste and judgment rather than technical execution alone.

### Surviving a Three-Decade Career

Kevin Lau's key to longevity: deliberate separation between being an "artist for hire" versus being an "artist." "I chose the path of designer, and that's work for hire. I'm not a fine artist. When I go to work, I do have to solve a problem for a client."

The paradox of the successful creative: you must be simultaneously passionate and dispassionate. Care deeply enough to push for excellence, but not so much that you lose yourself when the client changes or kills the work.

"One of the things that's hard not to do is take this thing personally." The biggest talent in the creative industry is not making great work -- it is developing objectivity about the work.

### Crystal Ball: Two Futures

**Lau's pragmatic view:** AI becomes another tool in the standard toolkit. "We still have to brainstorm, we still have to get in a room, we still have to bounce ideas off each other." The fundamental process of solving a client's brief remains; execution gets faster.

**Do's radical view:** A future where the creative director simply talks to the machine. "Read the brief that the client sent over. What are the potential two target markets?... What art styles make the most sense?... Generate 35 ideas. Write 14 scripts." The human's role is purely strategic and conversational -- a high-level dialogue with an infinitely capable creative partner.

**The irony:** As digital creation becomes dematerialized and infinitely reproducible, value swings back to the tangible. Live experiences, physical craftsmanship, things that cannot be replicated by AI. Lau points to the billions generated by Taylor Swift's Eras Tour. That is an experience you must physically attend.

## Positions and Opinions

- AI threatens execution-layer creatives, not strategy-layer creatives. The dividing line is judgment and taste vs. technical skill.
- Taste is a learnable skill, not an innate gift. Built through exposure to art history, craftsmanship, and critical analysis.
- The creative director's role becomes more valuable, not less, in an AI world. You become the director giving the machine creative direction.
- Deep knowledge of art history and design principles is the practical advantage -- it lets you direct AI with precision rather than accepting generic output.
- Every major tool revolution (desktop publishing, digital cameras, web tools, motion design software) produced the same panic. The creatives who led with judgment survived and thrived. AI is the same pattern.
- As digital becomes commoditized, tangible and experiential value increases. The pendulum swings.
- Career longevity requires the ability to separate personal identity from client work. Professional objectivity is a skill more important than any technical skill.
