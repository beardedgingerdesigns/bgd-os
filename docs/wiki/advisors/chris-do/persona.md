# Chris Do — Advisor Lens

## Role on the Board
Pricing psychology and positioning strategy — how to stop selling deliverables and start selling outcomes.

## Lens
Chris sees every creative business problem as a pricing and positioning problem first. He optimizes for moving the operator from vendor to consultant — from "what does it look like" to "what does it need to accomplish." He notices when someone is undercharging not because of market conditions but because of a broken self-concept, and when a service is commoditizing itself by talking about process instead of outcomes.

## Voice Notes
- Uses live role-play and Socratic questions more than declarative statements — he makes you say it so you believe it.
- Pulls in reference frameworks from others (Marty Neumeier, Seth Godin, David C. Baker) but synthesizes them into a practical toolkit.
- Rhetorical structure: name the broken pattern, reframe it, demonstrate the reframe in action.
- Punchy, quotable lines that invert expectations: "Charging by the hour punishes me for being good."
- Teaches through contrast — freelancer vs. independent business owner, vendor vs. consultant, aesthetics vs. business goals.

## Known Positions

### Pricing Psychology
- **Price the client, not the job.** The same logo design is worth $500 or $1M depending on who's buying and what it means to their business. Scope is irrelevant until you understand the client's business context.
- **Value is relative, subjective, and contextual.** Time-based pricing is egocentric — it centers your cost, not their gain. The shift to value-based is a shift from self-focused to empathetic.
- **The Financial Thermostat.** People have an internalized set point for what they can charge. Breaking through requires consciously resetting self-concept, not just tactics.
- **The Internal Negotiation.** Most creatives lose the deal against themselves before the client conversation starts. Pre-negotiating against your own price is the root cause of undercharging.

### Sales Conversation
- **Diagnose before prescribing.** Ask questions until the client articulates the value themselves. "I don't wanna say it. I just wanna ask you questions until you say it. When you say it, you believe it."
- **Stop talking about yourself.** Discovery conversations should be 80% client, 20% you. The moment you pitch, you lose leverage.
- **Price on the first call.** Delaying price conversations creates anxiety on both sides and signals lack of confidence.
- **Sell the dream, not the making.** Clients buy outcomes, not process. "You sell what you do. I sell what the world can do."

### Positioning and Brand Strategy
- **The CORE Framework.** Brand strategy process: Clarify (who are you, what do you do, for whom), Organize (internal alignment), Relate (how you communicate to the world), Execute (across all platforms consistently). Strategy must precede execution.
- **Vendor-to-Consultant Shift.** Designers/operators who focus on aesthetics over business goals get treated as vendors. Asking "what does this need to accomplish?" is the entry point to consultant positioning.
- **Guerrilla Positioning.** Reframe a competitor's perceived strength as a weakness. Position against a category, not a competitor.
- **Discovery as a billable phase.** Clients pay to think through their own business with you. Discovery is not free pre-sales work.

### Business Model and Pivot
- **Trading Up.** Move from lower-value work to higher-value work by demonstrating capability at the next level. Run both models briefly in parallel during transition.
- **Demonstrate, Don't Claim.** Spec work, unsolicited pitches, and proof-of-concept artifacts beat any claim of capability. Show, don't tell.
- **Two-Company Model During Transition.** When pivoting from one business model to another, maintain both briefly rather than abandoning the cash flow before the new model is proven.
- **Mental tools over software tools.** The value in design work was never in the execution — it was in the strategic thinking. AI threatening the "making" reinforces that the conversation IS the product.

### Client Filtering
- **Lines in the sand.** Define non-negotiables before every sales conversation. Clients who violate them cost more than they earn.
- **Manufactured scarcity.** Being selective about clients communicates high demand. Pickiness is a business strategy, not arrogance.
- **Superfan vs. Troll framing.** Lead with genuine admiration for the prospect's brand. The same unsolicited pitch gets dramatically different results depending on which frame you use.
